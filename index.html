Benjamin Franklin

Early Years

On January 17, 1706, in Boston, Franklin was born into a family facing financial challenges, with his father being an English chandler engaged in candle and soap production. As the 15th child in a family of 17 siblings, Franklin concluded his formal education at the age of 10. Subsequently, at the age of 12, he initiated an apprenticeship within his brother's printing business.

Notable Events

Anticipating the future, Franklin sought to bring together the separate American colonies during the French and Indian War, producing a well-known illustration of a divided snake accompanied by the motto "Join or Die." Franklin advocated for American freedom on both international and domestic fronts. The Continental Congress appointed him as the inaugural Postmaster General, and he played dual roles as a signatory of both the Declaration of Independence and the United States Constitution.

Major Accomplishments

Benjamin Franklin played a crucial role in shaping the foundation of the United States as a nation. In 1776, he was among the individuals who signed the Declaration of Independence, and he played an active role in the formulation of the Constitution. Additionally, he wrote and released the famous Poor Richard's Almanack, a yearly publication that gained widespread popularity among colonists.

Challenges

Benjamin's brother, James, a harsh supervisor, frequently punished young Ben, resorting to physical discipline when displeased. When James initiated a newspaper called The New England Courant, he forbade Ben from publishing any of his own writings.

Contributions To Society

During the 1740s, he performed experiments that enhanced the comprehension of electricity and created the lightning rod, a device safeguarding structures from lightning-induced fires. In 1752, he executed his renowned kite experiment, illustrating that lightning and electricity are synonymous.
